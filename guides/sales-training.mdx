## The core principle

The difference between order-taking and consultative selling is **opinion**. Order-takers ask "what do you need?" and wait. Consultative sellers arrive with a point of view about the buyer's world, validate it through questions, and guide the conversation toward insights the buyer couldn't reach alone.

Your job isn't to pitch Mintlify. Your job is to understand how documentation actually functions inside a technology business and help buyers see what they're missing.

## What "opinion" looks like in practice

**Without opinion:**
"So tell me about your documentation challenges."

**With opinion:**
"Most DevRel teams I talk to spend 60-70% of their time fighting tooling instead of writing content. Where does your team fall on that spectrum?"

The second version does three things:

1. Demonstrates you understand their world
2. Anchors a benchmark they can react to
3. Opens a conversation about pain without asking them to do the work of identifying it

## The framework: hypothesis → question → depth

Every discovery question should follow this pattern:

| Step | What it does | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hypothesis** | Your informed belief about their situation | "Engineering teams maintaining docs infra resent it" |
| **Question** | Tests the hypothesis while surfacing pain | "How do your engineers feel about owning the docs stack?" |
| **Depth** | Follows the thread to quantify or clarify | "What would they build instead if they had that time back?" |

## Opinion-led questions by persona

### Engineering leaders

**Your hypotheses:**

- Engineers resent maintaining undifferentiated infrastructure
- Docs work is invisible until something breaks
- They underestimate the true cost of "free" open-source solutions

**Questions that test these:**

| Hypothesis | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Engineers don't want to own docs | "When new features are shipped, and teams are looking to kick-off a new sprint, what happens to the tribal knowledge of that feature?" |
| Hidden maintenance cost | "What's the ratio of time spent maintaining your docs stack versus actually improving content and executing on strategy?" |
| Underestimated total cost | "When you chose Docusaurus, did you budget for ongoing maintenance, or just initial setup?" |

**Follow the thread:**

- "If you could redeploy those engineers tomorrow, what would they work on?"
- "How does docs maintenance factor into your sprint planning?"

### DevRel / developer experience

**Your hypotheses:**

- Their docs don't reflect the quality of their product
- They can't prove documentation ROI to leadership
- They're understaffed and need leverage through tooling

**Questions that test these:**

| Hypothesis | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Docs quality gap | "When developers evaluate your product, does the docs experience match the product experience?" |
| Can't prove impact | "If leadership asked you tomorrow what documentation contributed to pipeline, could you answer?" |
| Need leverage | "How many people on your team own documentation? How does that compare to your product velocity?" |

**Follow the thread:**

- "What would change if you could see exactly which docs pages correlate with conversion?"
- "When product ships faster than you can document, what falls through the cracks?"

### Technical writers / docs leads

**Your hypotheses:**

- They're blocked by engineering dependencies
- They spend more time on process than writing
- They can't demonstrate their impact with data

**Questions that test these:**

| Hypothesis | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering dependency | "When you need to publish an urgent update, how many people have to touch it before it goes live?" |
| Process overhead | "What percentage of your week is actual writing versus tooling, reviews, and publishing mechanics?" |
| Can't prove impact | "When you advocate for more docs resources, what data do you bring to that conversation?" |

**Follow the thread:**

- "What would you do with an extra day per week?"
- "If you could publish without an engineering ticket, what would change?"

### Platform / internal KB teams

**Your hypotheses:**

- They're the "human search engine" for their organization
- Confluence is where knowledge goes to die
- People ask on Slack because search doesn't work

**Questions that test these:**

| Hypothesis | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Human search engine | "How many times per week does someone ping you directly instead of reading the docs?" |
| Confluence graveyard | "When someone searches Confluence, do they find the answer—or do they give up and ask a person?" |
| Slack over docs | "What percentage of questions in your team Slack could be answered by existing documentation?" |

**Follow the thread:**

- "What would you do with the hours you spend answering repeat questions?"
- "If documentation could answer questions 24/7, what changes for your team?"

### Support / success leaders

**Your hypotheses:**

- They're answering the same questions repeatedly
- They can't scale without hiring
- Self-serve is a strategy, not a nice-to-have

**Questions that test these:**

| Hypothesis | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Repeat questions | "If you pulled your top 20 tickets from last month, how many could a good doc have prevented?" |
| Scaling problem | "What's your plan for handling 2x ticket volume without 2x headcount?" |
| Self-serve gap | "When customers search your docs before filing a ticket, how often do they find the answer?" |

**Follow the thread:**

- "What's the cost per ticket including engineer escalations?"
- "If you deflected 30% of tickets, what would your team do with that time?"

## The AI-native angle

This is the opinion that differentiates you from every other docs conversation:

**The insight:** 48% of documentation traffic is now AI agents. Cursor, Copilot, Claude—your users are consuming docs through AI, not just browsers. Most documentation platforms were built for humans only.

**How to introduce it:**

"Here's something that might surprise you. Across our customer base, 48% of docs traffic is AI agents—Cursor, Copilot, Claude Code. That number is growing monthly. When your users ask an AI coding assistant about your product, where does that answer come from?"

**Follow-up questions:**

- "Do you know what percentage of your docs traffic is AI versus human?"
- "If a user asks Cursor how to implement your API, does it get the right answer?"
- "Does your current platform have an MCP server? Can AI tools connect directly to your docs?"

This plants a problem they probably haven't considered—and positions you as the person who sees where the market is going.

## Converting pain to urgency

Uncovering pain isn't enough. You need to attach **cost** and **timeline** to it.

### Quantifying pain:

| Pain | Quantifying question |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering time on docs | "What's the fully-loaded cost of the engineers who touch docs? Half an FTE at $200K is $100K/year." |
| Support tickets | "How many tickets per month? At $15/ticket, 1,000 tickets is $15K/month in support cost." |
| Pipeline impact | "What percentage of your pipeline touches docs before converting? If it's 40% of $5M, we're talking about $2M influenced by docs quality." |

### Creating timeline pressure:

| Trigger | Question |
| --- | --- |
| New product launch | "When does the new product ship? What's the plan for docs?" |
| Headcount constraints | "If you can't hire more support reps, how do you handle growth?" |
| Competitive pressure | "When you lose deals to [competitor], what do they say about your docs?" |
| Contract renewal | "When does your current docs contract expire?" |

## Practice scenarios

### Scenario 1: Engineering leader at a Series B startup

They built docs on Docusaurus two years ago. One engineer maintains it part-time. They're about to launch a new product.

**Your hypotheses:**

- That "part-time" engineer is spending more time than they admit
- The new product launch will break their current docs workflow
- They haven't thought about AI agent traffic

**Questions to run:**

1. "How much time is your engineer actually spending on docs maintenance versus what was planned?"
2. "When the new product launches, who's going to document it? Same engineer?"
3. "Do you know what percentage of your docs traffic is AI agents versus humans?"

### Scenario 2: DevRel lead at a developer tools company

They have a small team (2-3 people) and can't keep up with product velocity. They've complained about their current solution but haven't prioritized a change.

**Your hypotheses:**

- They're embarrassed by the gap between product quality and docs quality
- They can't prove impact, so they can't get resources
- The AI agent shift gives them a new angle to advocate for change

**Questions to run:**

1. "When a developer evaluates your product, does the docs experience match what they'll get from the actual product?"
2. "If you went to leadership tomorrow asking for docs investment, what data would you bring?"
3. "48% of docs traffic is now AI agents. That's a new story you can tell leadership—are you equipped to tell it?"

### Scenario 3: Platform team lead at an enterprise company

They manage internal documentation across multiple tools. Engineers complain that they can't find anything. Leadership wants to reduce the "tap on shoulder" culture.

**Your hypotheses:**

- They're the human search engine and they hate it
- Confluence isn't working but no one wants to admit it
- AI-powered search would change their life

**Questions to run:**

1. "How many times per week does someone ping you directly instead of searching the docs?"
2. "When someone searches Confluence, what's their success rate? Do they find the answer or give up?"
3. "If an AI could answer questions 24/7 with accurate, cited responses, what would change for your team?"

## The outcome

When a rep arrives with opinion, three things happen:

1. **The buyer feels understood.** You're not asking them to explain their world—you already know it.
2. **The conversation goes deeper.** Instead of surface-level "what are your challenges," you're discussing specific metrics, workflows, and decisions.
3. **You're positioned as a strategic partner.** You're not selling software—you're helping them see their situation more clearly.

That's the difference between a vendor and a trusted advisor. That's how you productize sales.
